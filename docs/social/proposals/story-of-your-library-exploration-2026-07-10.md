# The story of your library — exploration

The music-history learning vision, anchored where it becomes irresistible:
*their* songs. Not "a history of shoegaze" in the abstract, but "you have 214
shoegaze likes — here is where that sound came from, and your library's own
earliest and purest examples of it." Education that starts from love already
declared.

Sibling of `living-playlists-and-membership-exploration-2026-07-10.md` (the
membership frame) and the long-term reason the product's design language
matters: a reading surface built to make learning feel good.

## The problem this solves

Music history content exists everywhere (Wikipedia, YouTube essays, RYM) but
it is unanchored — you read about a scene and it stays abstract. The unlock
hearted has is the join: every user carries a corpus of proof (their likes)
that the history is *about them*. "The song you liked in 2019 is a direct
descendant of this 1988 record" turns a lesson into a mirror. Nothing else
holds both the history and the user's declared taste.

Strategically, it is also the membership's depth story: living playlists are
the utility perk; this is the *love* perk — the thing that makes the
Backstage Pass feel like a club about music rather than a feature flag.

## What already exists

- **`song_analysis` JSONB** — per-song qualitative analyses (gemini-2.5-flash,
  ~9,000 songs and growing) already discuss sound, lineage, and context; the
  raw material for connecting a user's songs to a narrative.
- **Genres on `song`** — the user's genre distribution locates them on any
  lineage map; Phase-1, so free-tier available.
- **The lens vocabulary work** (`docs/analysis/lens-vocabulary.md`) — prior
  thinking about a controlled descriptive vocabulary that a lineage graph
  could hang off.
- **The commons economics**: history content about a genre/scene/era is
  cacheable *globally*, exactly like `song_analysis` — written once, read by
  every user whose library touches it. The subsidy math from
  `v1_hearted_brand/docs/shared-analysis-cache-subsidy.md` applies with even
  better ratios, since the genre vocabulary is finite and Zipf-concentrated.

## Mechanics

1. **Content units: chapters keyed by genre/scene/era** — a global
   `genre_chapter`-style table (essay + structured lineage edges:
   influenced-by, reacted-against, sibling-scene). Generated by LLM **once
   per unit**, then cached forever and editorially reviewable; cost is cents
   per chapter across a finite vocabulary, not per user.
2. **Accuracy is the hard problem, treated as one.** Music history is full of
   contested claims and hallucination bait. Chapters are generated with
   citation requirements, flagged confidence, and a human-review pass before
   going live (the corpus is small enough — hundreds of chapters, not
   millions). Ship slowly; wrong history in a learning product is fatal to
   trust in a way a bad playlist isn't.
3. **Personalization is the join, not the content**: a user's "story" is the
   chapter graph *entered through their distribution* — start at their
   densest genres, walk edges outward. Their own songs are inlined as the
   examples ("your earliest like in this lineage: …" via `liked_at` +
   genres; deeper song-level connective tissue via `song_analysis` for
   members).
4. **Exploration frontier**: edges pointing *outside* the library ("shoegaze
   came from this; you have nothing from there — listen") are the discovery
   path, and pair naturally with taste neighbors (whose libraries hold what
   yours lacks).

## How it should look

- **A reading-first surface** — the design-to-facilitate-learning ambition:
  generous type, one idea per screen, the lineage as a walkable map rather
  than a wall of prose. Closer to a beautifully-set book chapter with your
  records embedded in the margins than to a feed.
- **Entry points**: from a genre pill anywhere in the app ("what *is* this?"),
  from a song's analysis, and a "Your story" home that picks tonight's
  chapter from your densest unexplored genre.
- **Each chapter ends in a playlist**: "the lineage, in your songs" — a
  seeded draft mixing their likes with canonical examples. Learning that
  terminates in listening.

## Tier placement

**Free**: one chapter per genre at taste level — enough to prove the magic
and make genre pills feel alive. **Member**: the full walk — deep chapters,
song-level connections through their own analyses, the exploration frontier,
lineage playlists. This is deliberately the *most* member-weighted feature
on the growth list: it is pure depth, has real editorial cost, and is the
clearest answer to "what does the club give me that a tool doesn't."

## How it strengthens the concept

It is the feature that makes "membership club about the love of music" true
rather than aspirational — utility features justify the price, this one
justifies the identity. It compounds the commons (chapters are shared
infrastructure), gives `song_analysis` a second life beyond matching, and
differentiates on an axis (learning, editorial care) that neither Spotify
nor last.fm competes on at all.

## Open questions

- Editorial model: pure LLM-with-review, LLM-draft + human-rewrite, or
  eventually commissioned writing? (Cost says start with reviewed LLM; the
  brand's voice guide should govern the prose either way.)
- Vocabulary spine: build the lineage graph on the existing genre tags, the
  lens vocabulary, or a curated merge? Genre tags are noisy at the edges.
- Scope discipline for v0: genres only? Scenes and eras multiply the graph;
  a v0 of ~50 head genres covers most libraries' density.
- Citations UX: inline sources in a consumer reading surface — footnotes,
  a "sources" drawer, or trust-by-review with no visible apparatus?
- How does progress work — is "walking the map" tracked (read states,
  frontier visualization) without gamifying it into streaks?
